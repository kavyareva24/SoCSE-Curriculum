# B.Tech Curriculum Strategy — AI-Era Readiness

**Scope:** CSE, ISE, IT, AIML, AIDS, IoT and Cyber Security (with Blockchain)
**Context:** REVA University, School of Computer Science & Engineering

---

## 1. Purpose

To define a curriculum strategy that prepares students for the job roles that will actually exist when they graduate — accounting for AI disruption to entry-level software work — while honestly fitting the students REVA admits, not an idealized cohort.

---

## 2. The core problem

AI disruption hits this curriculum in a specific, asymmetric way:

- The entry-level coding, testing, and support roles that REVA's majority traditionally relied on are exactly the roles being automated first.
- The roles that survive — AI-augmented product builders, domain specialists, people who can orchestrate agentic systems — demand the hands-on judgment that is hardest to teach to a disengaged cohort.

So two design constraints pull against each other:

> The **jobs** are moving up the skill ladder, while the **students** are mostly not self-driven.

---

## 3. Student reality (planning assumptions, never labels)

These figures describe the cohort the curriculum must serve. They are used to **resource** the system — how much scaffolding to build, how many seats per track — and are **never** used to pre-assign any individual student to a path.

| Dimension | Distribution |
|---|---|
| CET rank | ~30% under 25,000 rank; ~70% with graduating marks above 45 |
| Background | ~30% rural; ~40% from a biology background |
| Engagement persona | ~10% Explorers, ~20% Achievers, ~40% Passengers, ~30% Resistors |

## 4. What the 2026 scheme already encodes (reverse-engineered strategy)

Read as a strategic artifact, the existing scheme already contains five pillars:

1. **The W1–W8 workshop spine** — eight 0-0-2 practical (P-only) courses, two per semester across semesters 3–6 (Portfolio Development, Web Design, Advanced DBMS, Data Engineering, Data Visualization, MLOps, Big Data, AI App Dev). This is the "hands-on time / 2 workshops per semester" pillar, already built in — theory (lecture-heavy) is deliberately separated from doing (practice-only).
2. **The AEC placement spine** — AEC-1 through AEC-4 ("Placement Training") run every semester from 3 to 6 as steady 1-credit drip, rather than a final-year crash course. This is the "pass the entrance test" objective made literal.
3. **PE1–PE6 elective baskets** — clustered into an *industry-vertical* track (DS for BFSI / Energy / Aerospace, DS for functions) and a *frontier-AI* track (LLM Engineering, LLMOps, Agentic AI, Multimodal LLM, Large Action Models, Adversarial/Ethical AI).
4. **A project ladder** — Mini Project 1 (sem 5) → Mini Project 2 (sem 6) → Capstone Phase I + Internship (sem 7) → Capstone Phase II + Internship (sem 8). Credits intentionally drop in the final year (sem 7 = 18, sem 8 = 13) to empty space for doing over teaching.
5. **AI threaded from year one** — AI Foundations (sem 2), Advanced C *with Generative AI* (sem 2), then a clean theory escalator: Principles of AI → ML → DL → NLP → Generative AI.

## 5. Important aspects

Each works *with* existing structure — most are governance, ownership, or assessment changes rather than new courses.

### 5.1 W1–W8 become the just-in-time currency mechanism

The workshop titles in the scheme are **indicative placeholders, not commitments**. Professional-elective (pod) teams design the actual workshop content **just-in-time** so it reflects the technology landscape at the moment students take it. The scheme stays stable on paper (credits, slots fixed); content floats freely underneath.

This is the single highest-leverage element because workshops are the only lever fast enough to retrofit the batches graduating soonest.

### 5.2 PE baskets become pods that own the full arc

Today, electives, workshops, mini-projects, and internships are administered separately. The enhancement re-draws ownership lines so each **pod team owns a complete two-year track**: the professional electives, the just-in-time workshops, the projects, and the internships in its domain — as one coherent sequence taught and assessed by one faculty team. No new courses; ownership is consolidated around courses that already exist.

Pod domains map to the program's target outcomes:

- Agentic AI software product development (Enterprise Applications)
- AI/ML and Data Engineering
- IoT, Blockchain, and process automation for enterprises
- Cybersecurity services

### 5.3 Student choice into tracks, gated by management

The pod defines its track / projects / internships / workshops as a **bundle**, and students **choose** into it, **subject to management approval**. The pod is therefore not just an administrative owner but a *menu the student selects from*.


### 5.4 Performance feedback steers track choice; entrepreneurship is a narrow exit

How a student performs in core courses generates **feedback** that advises which professional tracks suit them. Sorting thus happens **late and with data**, at the track-selection boundary — never early and with labels.

The mainline destination for ~95% of students is **employment** as a contributor-grade product/service worker. **Entrepreneurship is a narrow exit (~5%)** — a branch off the end of the progression path for a small, self-selecting group, not the summit everyone climbs toward. The final-year capstone can *be* a venture for that cohort (same credits, same slot, different deliverable) without distorting the majority's curriculum.

## 6. Progression model

```
Common core courses (CSCore)
  same course for all — awareness level + advanced level, separated by assessment
        │
        ▼
Performance feedback
  how you did → which tracks suit you
        │
        ▼
Track choice (pod menu)
  student selects · management approves
        │
        ▼
Pod-owned tracks (workshops + projects + internship, designed just-in-time)
  Agentic AI / Enterprise apps  ·  AI–ML / Data Engineering
  IoT / Blockchain / Automation  ·  Cybersecurity services
        │
        ▼
Employment — mainline (~95%)  ───►  Entrepreneurship — narrow exit (~5%)
contributor-grade product/service work
```

A student's day is split between **CSCore** (the placement-readiness floor, continuous-assessment driven) and **hands-on time** (workshops, projects, internships owned by the pods).

---

## 7. Success metrics

The intended outcome is **placement-weighted** (employability of the ~95% mainline), with entrepreneurship treated as a bonus exit for ~5%, not a primary KPI. Specific numeric targets — placement rate, share of students shipping a product, ventures spun out — remain to be set and will determine where energy is spent across the Passenger and Explorer cohorts.
